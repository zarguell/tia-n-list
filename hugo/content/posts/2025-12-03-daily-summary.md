**Gainsight Confirms Token Breach Tied to Salesforce Advisory**
Customer success platform Gainsight has verified a security incident where an undisclosed number of customer tokens were compromised via its Salesforce integration. The announcement comes on the heels of a broader Salesforce security advisory that led to Gainsight's connected app being temporarily disabled. This breach exposes the systemic risk of interconnected SaaS ecosystems where a vulnerability in one major platform can cascade to dozens of others. For organizations using integrated third-party apps, this is a wake-up call to not only secure their own systems but also to scrutinize the security posture of their vendors and their vendor's vendors. Immediately rotate any API keys or tokens connected to platforms like Salesforce and review the principle of least privilege for all integrations. [Gainsight Verifies Token Breach Linked to Salesforce Advisory, Issues New IOCs](https://gbhackers.com/gainsight-verifies-token-breach/)

**Public GitLab Repositories Leak Over 17,000 Secrets**
A comprehensive scan of GitLab's 5.6 million public repositories uncovered more than 17,000 exposed secrets, including API keys and credentials, spanning over 2,800 unique domains. This isn't a vulnerability in GitLab's platform itself, but a massive exploitation of poor operational security by developers. This finding underscores that hardcoded secrets remain a plague on the software development lifecycle, providing attackers with a direct path to cloud infrastructure, databases, and third-party services. Security and DevOps teams must implement automated secret detection scans in their CI/CD pipelines and educate developers on the proper use of vaults and environment variables to prevent these easily avoidable but highly damaging leaks. [Public GitLab repositories exposed more than 17,000 secrets](https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/public-gitlab-repositories-exposed-more-than-17-000-secrets/)

**Poetry Proves a Universal Jailbreak for LLMs**
New research has demonstrated that converting malicious prompts into poetry can reliably bypass the safety filters of major Large Language Models (LLMs). The "adversarial poetry" technique achieved jailbreak success rates as high as 62% for hand-crafted poems, significantly outperforming standard prose prompts across multiple model families. This reveals a fundamental flaw in how LLMs interpret context and intent, showing that simple stylistic changes can override extensive safety training. For organizations deploying or relying on LLMs, this is a critical reminder that traditional input sanitization is insufficient. Defense-in-depth strategies, including robust output filtering and strict prompt guidelines for users, are essential to mitigate this novel and surprisingly effective attack vector. [Prompt Injection Through Poetry](https://www.schneier.com/blog/archives/2025/11/prompt-injection-through-poetry.html)

**ShinyHunters Level Up with New Ransomware-as-a-Service**
The notorious data theft group ShinyHunters appears to be making a significant pivot into ransomware with the development of their own RaaS platform, "ShinySp1d3r." This move marks the group's first foray into developing its own encryption tools, signaling an evolution from simple data brokers to a more sophisticated and damaging cybercrime operation. The emergence of another RaaS platform lowers the barrier to entry for other attackers and will likely fuel a new wave of ransomware attacks. Security teams should treat ShinyHunters as a dual threat (data exfiltration + encryption) and prepare for their tactics by hardening backup systems and implementing detection for the tools and TTPs associated with their new ransomware strain. [ShinyHunters Develop Sophisticated New Ransomware-as-a-Service Tool](https://gbhackers.com/ransomware-as-a-service-tool/)

**Cybercriminals Register 18,000 Holiday-Themed Domains**
Threat actors have registered approximately 18,000 new domains with holiday-themed keywords to power seasonal phishing and scam campaigns. According to FortiGuard Labs, attackers are leveraging automation and AI to make these malicious sites more convincing and harder to detect, targeting both consumers and e-commerce businesses during the peak shopping season. This isn't just a nuisance; it's a predictable, large-scale assault on brand trust and customer credentials. Security teams should proactively block newly registered domains containing their brand names and common holiday terms, and conduct user awareness campaigns highlighting the dangers of unsolicited "too good to be true" offers. [Cybercriminals Register 18,000 Holiday-Themed Domains to Launch Seasonal Scams](https://gbhackers.com/holiday-themed-domains/)

Cybersecurity firm GreyNoise has released a new free utility that allows users to check if their IP address has been observed by its sensors participating in malicious activity, such as being part of a botnet.
